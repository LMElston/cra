CRA scam alerts

The Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) is regularly made aware of scams impersonating the CRA. Stay up-to-date with the CRA's scam alert to help avoid scams that may target you.

If you suspect you may be a victim of fraud or have been tricked into giving personal or financial information, visit the section on what to do on the Protect yourself against fraud page.

Latest scam alerts

Phishing text message offering refund

What is it: A phishing text message scam impersonating the CRA to offer refunds to Canadians, targeting any Canadian year round.

How to recognize it: Scammers are using a method known as spoofing to hide their number and use a different number on your phone display. They will claim to be the CRA and offer a refund, encouraging you to click on a link provided in their message. See an example of this type of text message.

If you click on the message, you may be asked to provide personal information such as your SIN, date of birth or your name. Then you will be asked to provide your online banking information to accept the refund by e-transfer.

What to do: If you receive a text message from the CRA, do not reply. The CRA will not use text messages or instant messages to start a conversation with you about your refund. Find more information about what the CRA will or will not do, and how to protect yourself against fraud.

Fraudulent tax returns – identity theft

What is it: Bad actors target Canadians, and use your user ID and password to file fraudulent tax returns. This type of identity theft can target all Canadians.

A scammer could file a fraudulent income tax return with personal information, and could change a personal address or direct deposit information.

How to recognize: Scammers may use different methods to gain personal information. You may notice a change on your account, or receive an email notification saying your account has been modified without your knowledge.

What to do: If you notice a change on your account has been made, and you did not make it, contact the CRA as soon as possible. Find more information on how to protect yourself against fraud

Extortion phone call

What is it: Extortion phone calls from scammers claiming to be from the CRA, targeting any Canadian, year round.

How to recognize: Scammers are using a method known as spoofing to hide their number and use a different number on your phone display. They may use a local number which will appear on your call display, or use numbers from local law enforcement agencies or governments. At the beginning of the call, the scam may ask you to confirm your SIN. They will then ask to receive payment from you by Bitcoin or by gift cards. See an example of a call from a scammer.

What to do: The CRA will not use aggressive language or demand immediate payment by Bitcoin, prepaid credit cards or gift cards. Find more information about what the CRA will or will not do, and how to protect yourself against fraud.

Phishing email offering refund

What is it: Email phishing scams where scammers are impersonating the CRA to offer refunds to Canadians, targeting any Canadian, year round.

How to recognize: Scammers will add a legitimate email CRA address as the display name. In the email, they will tell you the CRA needs to send a refund, and to click on the link provided. If you click on the link, you will be asked to provide personal information such as your SIN, date of birth or your name. Then you will be asked to provide your online banking information to accept the refund by e-transfer. See an example of this type of email phishing scam.

The CRA will not ask you to provide personal information by email or send requests for e-transfers. Find more information about what the CRA will or will not do, and how to protect yourself against fraud.

What to do: If you hover (do not click) your computer mouse over the name of the sender, or type the email into your web browser, you may be able to identify where the email address is coming from. Canadian Emergency Response Benefit - identity theft

What is it: Bad actors target Canadians who have applied for the Canadian Emergency Response Benefit, and use their information to apply for the benefit.

How to recognize it: If you notice any suspicious activity on your CRA accounts. Bad actors can also gain access to your accounts when personal information is given to a scammer by text message or by phone.

What to do: The CRA will not ask for personal information by text message, and will not use aggressive language with you. If you're not sure if the CRA has contacted you, do not reply to the text or end the call and call us:
