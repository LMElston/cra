Cumulative effect eligibility

On this page

  • Overview
  • Eligibility criteria checklist

Overview

Some people may be limited by mental or physical impairments, but do not meet the eligibility criteria for a marked restriction.

The cumulative effect category lets you combine the effects of limitations in 2 or more categories if, together, their effect is as severe as having a marked restriction in 1 category.

For example: If a person always takes a long time to walk and dress, and the extra time it takes to do these 2 activities is equal to being unable (or taking 3 times longer) to do just 1 of them, then they may be eligible.

What marked restriction means
  • You are unable to do the activity or it takes 3 times longer, even with the use of appropriate therapy, medications, and devices
  • This restriction exists all or almost all of the time (generally at least 90%)
  • The restriction has lasted or is expected to last for 12 consecutive months
What significant limitation means
  • The 2 or more limitations exist together all or almost all of the time (generally at least 90%)
  • If the limitations were combined, their cumulative effect (combined impact) would be both of the following:
    • equivalent to being unable, or taking 3 times longer, to do the activity of 1 of the categories
    • present all or almost all of the time (generally at least 90%), even with the use of appropriate therapy, medications, and devices

Review the criteria for marked restrictions in the categories below. If you have limitations in 2 or more of these categories, you may combine their effects to be equivalent to 1 marked restriction:

  • Walking
  • Mental functions
  • Dressing
  • Feeding
  • Eliminating (bowel or bladder functions)
  • Speaking
  • Hearing
  • Vision
  • Life-sustaining therapy (not included)
Example of people who may be eligible

Evelyne is her mother Joan's caregiver

Evelyne is the caregiver to her mother, Joan, who lives with her.

For years, Joan was able to manage on her own even though she had a progressive medical condition. But her condition slowly became worse and now she struggles to walk, dress, and feed herself.

Evelyne applied for the DTC for her mother. As a result of the information provided by the medical practitioner on the application form, Joan is now eligible for DTC in the cumulative effect category because she needs constant care and supervision.

As Joan has little taxable income and her daughter helps with her food, clothing, and shelter throughout the year, Evelyne may claim any unused portion of the tax credit.

If you’re not sure you qualify

This does not guarantee eligibility. When you apply, the CRA will base its decision on your medical practitioner’s assessment.
