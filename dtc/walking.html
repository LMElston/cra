Overview

For some people, walking can be a challenge even with the help of appropriate therapy, medication, or devices. The DTC aims to offset some of the costs caused by care, treatment, and related activities by reducing the amount of income tax you pay.

Eligibility for the DTC is based on the effects of the impairment, not a diagnosis or the presence of a condition.

Example of people who may be eligible

Marie injured her leg and has to use a wheelchair

Marie and Joe are both retired.

Ten years ago, Marie fell down a ladder and injured her right leg. She now has to use a wheelchair to get around outside her home.

Marie applied for the DTC. As a result of the information provided by the medical practitioner on the application form, she is now eligible for the tax credit.

Marie also got a big income tax refund for the last 10 years because her medical practitioner was able to certify that her impairment started 10 years ago.

Being eligible for the DTC also means Marie may be eligible for other government programs for persons with disabilities.

Option 1

What marked restriction means

You may have a marked restriction in 1 or more of the eligibility categories if:

  • You are unable to do the activity or it takes 3 times longer, even with the use of appropriate therapy, medications, and devices
  • This restriction exists all or almost all of the time (at least 90%)
  • The restriction has lasted or is expected to last for 12 consecutive months
What significant limitations means

You may have a significant limitation in 2 or more of the eligibility categories (except life-sustaining therapy) if:

  • The limitations exist together all or almost all of the time (at least 90%)
  • If the limitations were combined, their combined impact would be:
    • equivalent to being unable, or taking 3 times longer, to do the activity of one of the categories
    • present all or almost all of the time (at least 90%), even with the use of appropriate therapy, medications, and devices
