Mental functions

On this page

Overview

For some people, performing everyday mental functions can be a challenge even with the help of appropriate therapy, medication, and devices. The DTC aims to offset some of the costs caused by care, treatment, and related activities by reducing the amount of income tax you may to pay.

Eligibility for the DTC is based on the effects of an impairment, not a diagnosis or the presence of a condition.

Everyday mental functions include:

  • Adaptive functioning
    • abilities related to self-care, health, and safety
    • initiating and responding to social interactions
    • common, simple transactions, such as grocery shopping or paying a bill
  • Memory
    • the ability to remember simple instructions, basic personal information (such as a date of birth or address), or things that are important or of interest
  • Judgment, problem-solving, and goal-setting taken together, such as following treatment prescribed by a doctor, or selecting clothing that is appropriate to the weather
Example of people who may be eligible

Christie can only concentrate for short periods of time

Cheryl and Christie have been together for 30 years.

Two years ago, Cheryl noticed Christie was having trouble remembering things. Christie could only concentrate for short periods of time, and was confused by simple things like grocery shopping.

Christie applied for the DTC. As a result of the information provided by the medical practitioner on the application form, she is now eligible for the tax credit because she qualifies under the mental functions category.

As Christie has little taxable income, Cheryl may claim any unused portion of the tax credit.

Being eligible for the DTC also means Christie may be eligible for other government programs for persons with disabilities.

If you’re not sure you qualify

You should still apply even if you're not sure. We base your eligibility on your medical practitioner's assessment.

Or you may be eligible under the cumulative effect category, which lets you combine the effects of 2 less severe impairments.
